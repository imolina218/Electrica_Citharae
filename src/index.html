The creation

To compete with more high-end instruments from other manufacturers—particularly Gibsons Les Paul, introduced in 1952 in response to what Randall once called the “plain Jane” Telecaster—the Stratocaster was a marked step up in design and innovation for Fender. It had not one or two but three pickups, with switching and controls that created great tonal versatility (although, curiously, the switching configuration allowed only three of several possible pickup combinations).

Other Stratocaster innovations included single-coil alnico 3 pickups with staggered-height pole pieces that effectively addressed the varying output of the heavy string gauges in use at the time, and a slanting output jack mounted on the face of the guitar rather than the side.
The guitars distinctively shaped headstock—more fully curvaceous than that of the Telecaster and Precision Bass—was undoubtedly influenced by instruments built by Paul Bigsby and, like the Telecaster and Precision Bass before it, had all the tuners within easy reach on one side. The Stratocasters electronics were mounted to the single-ply white plastic pickguard (anodized aluminum on some early models) rather than to the body, enabling an entire pickguard/pickups/controls assembly to be dropped in place and screwed on (using eight screws) and only requiring connection to the output jack.

As design refinements preceded throughout late 1953 and early 1954, Leo and his staff were ably abetted by several western swing guitarists in addition to Gallion and Carson who were happy to field-test Stratocaster prototypes in local nightclubs. Their input was invaluable, and while the Stratocaster was in one sense Leo baby through and through, it was in another sense the collaborative work of a remarkable group of designers and musicians.

The creation

The story of the Les Paul’s genesis is surprisingly protracted. While Les Paul himself saw the potential of a solid bodied electric guitar as far back as 1945, it took seven years for Gibson president Ted McCarty to share his vision. And even then, the first Les Pauls that arrived on the market were very different beasts from the one we now know.

When we think of the solid-body electric guitar boom, we inevitably think of the early-to-mid 1950s. But the story of the Les Paul goes back further. In 1945, Les Paul – already a respected guitar innovator – presented Gibson with his idea for a solid-body Spanish guitar.

Gibson wanted a well-made, expensive instrument that fit their reputation as a high-end instrument manufacturer – a clone of the Tele this was not. In late 1951, they approached Les Paul with a prototype of their new instrument, and an enquiry; they wanted to imprint Les’ name on the headstock to increase sales.

It would be 1957 before the final component of the classic Gibson Les Paul was put in place. The P90s of earlier models were replaced with new “humbucker” pickups designed by Gibson.

Place in History

In 1958, Gibson phased out the Gold Top finish, replacing it with
their enduring Sunburst design. Yet, these sunburst Les Pauls,
produced between 1958 and 1960, were not a hit with consumers. A
mere 1700 units were sold in those two years, prompting Gibson to
drop them altogether in 1961.

In 1964, Keith Richards purchased a 1959 Sunburst instrument, becoming
the first guitarist on the British scene to use one. His peers followed
suit. Eric Clapton switched to a Les Paul in 1965, inspired by Freddie
King. By the end of the 1960s, Peter Green, Jeff Beck and Jimmy Page
were bearing the charge of the Les Paul resurgence.

The Les Paul had a tough time trying to be what it is today, but that
doesn't make it any less of a stratocaster. It was and still is wielded by
some of the best rock and roll bands and artists, without ever losing the touch that
was meant to have.
